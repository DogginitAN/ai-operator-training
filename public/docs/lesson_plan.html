Session 1: Becoming an AI Operator

Coaching lesson plan for Matt — HouseAccount
Friday 10:00 AM ~60 minutes Session 1 of ongoing

Key Statements — Repeat These Throughout

01
"The models are intelligence. When you're doing something that requires thinking, that's a signal to test a model with it."
This reframes AI from "tool" to "coworker." Every time he catches himself reasoning through a problem alone — drafting, analyzing, deciding — that's an opportunity to hand it to Claude.
02
"Brain, Hands, Memory. Claude thinks. Claude Code acts. CLAUDE.md remembers."
The three-layer model. Repeat this every time you introduce a new concept so it anchors to the same framework.
03
"If you're doing it more than twice, it should be a prompt. If it's a prompt you run more than twice, it should be automated."
The automation ladder: manual → prompt → saved prompt → script → agent. He doesn't need to get to "agent" on day one. Just start noticing the repetition.
04
"Context is everything. A model with no context is smart but useless. A model with your business context is a teammate."
This motivates writing CLAUDE.md files, creating Projects, and feeding Claude real documents. The model's ceiling is gated by what you give it to work with.
05
"You're not learning a tool. You're building a new reflex."
The outcome of coaching isn't "Matt knows Claude." It's "Matt's first instinct is to delegate to AI before doing it manually." That takes reps, not tutorials.
06
"The best AI operator isn't the best prompter. It's the person who knows what to hand off and what to keep."
Judgment > technique. Knowing WHEN to use AI matters more than knowing HOW. This is why the founder has to learn it themselves — it's a judgment call that requires domain expertise.
0–10 min Setup Check & Calibration

Goal: Confirm his tools work, understand where he's starting from.

Walk me through what you got set up from the guide. What worked? What didn't? What did you try on your own?

This tells you immediately whether he's at "I installed everything and played around" or "I skimmed the PDF." Adjust the rest of the call accordingly.

If anything isn't installed, fix it now. Share screen and walk through it together. Don't let broken tooling eat the call — get to claude running in his terminal before moving on.
What does your typical day look like at HouseAccount? Not the ideal — the actual. Where do you spend your time?

Listen for: repetitive tasks, decision bottlenecks, things that require him personally, things that shouldn't require him personally. You're building a mental map of where AI fits.

10–20 min The Mental Model — Intelligence on Tap

Goal: Shift his frame from "AI as tool" to "AI as intelligence I can direct."

Think about what a model actually is. It's not a search engine. It's not autocomplete. It's a compressed version of human reasoning that you can invoke instantly, at zero marginal cost, on any problem. When you find yourself thinking — analyzing, comparing, drafting, deciding — that's exactly when you should be testing a model with that same problem.
Here's the shift: most people ask "can AI do this?" The AI-native operator asks "why would I do this myself?"

The Delegation Test — walk him through this framework:

  • Does it require thinking? → Claude can probably do it, or at least get you 80% there
  • Is it repetitive? → It should definitely be a prompt, maybe a script
  • Does it require YOUR specific judgment? → You review, Claude drafts
  • Does it require real-time human relationship? → That stays with you. Everything else is negotiable.
The four things that stay human: relationships, taste, judgment on edge cases, and final accountability. Everything else is fair game.
20–35 min Exercise 1: Real Problem, Live

Goal: He watches you solve a REAL HouseAccount problem with Claude, then does one himself.

Give me something real. A provider email you need to respond to. A pricing question. A customer situation. Something you'd normally spend 10-15 minutes on.

YOUR demo (5 min): Take whatever he gives you and solve it live in Claude Desktop. Narrate your thinking out loud:

  • "I'm going to paste this email and give Claude context about who the provider is..."
  • "Watch how I'm not asking it to 'write an email' — I'm giving it the situation and letting it decide the approach..."
  • "See how it got 90% right? I'm going to tweak this one line and send it. That took 45 seconds instead of 10 minutes."
I didn't prompt it perfectly. I just gave it the problem and enough context. The model fills in the gaps. Your job isn't to write perfect prompts — it's to get the problem into the model's context window.

HIS turn (5 min): Now hand him the keyboard.

Have him pick a second real task — something he was going to do today anyway. Watch him do it in Claude. Coach in real-time: "give it more context about why this matters" or "try telling it the tone you want" or "just paste the whole email chain, don't summarize."
Don't over-coach the prompt. The instinct will be to help him write the "right" prompt. Let him fumble. The learning is in seeing that even a rough prompt gets useful output. Perfectionism about prompting is the #1 thing that prevents adoption.
35–45 min Exercise 2: Building Context (CLAUDE.md)

Goal: He creates his first Project and CLAUDE.md — the "memory" layer that makes Claude a HouseAccount expert.

Right now, Claude is smart but it knows nothing about HouseAccount. Every time you start a conversation, it's a blank slate. That's like hiring a brilliant consultant and never giving them any background docs. CLAUDE.md is the onboarding packet.
Open Claude Desktop → Projects → Create "HouseAccount." Together, write the project instructions. Have him dictate while you type (or vice versa). Cover:

• What HouseAccount does in 2-3 sentences
• Who the customers are (homeowners) and providers
• The team structure (who does what)
• The four agents they've scoped (Provider Sales, Customer Intake, Pricing Estimate, Concierge)
• Current pain points or bottlenecks
• Any terminology specific to HouseAccount

Then test it immediately:

Inside that Project, ask: "Based on what you know about HouseAccount, what's the single highest-leverage process I should automate first and why?" — show him how the context changes the quality of the response.
See the difference? Same model, same intelligence — but now it's reasoning about YOUR business, not generic advice. Context is the multiplier. The more you feed it, the better it gets.
45–52 min Exercise 3: The Batch Mindset

Goal: Introduce the pattern of batching repetitive work through Claude.

Here's where the compounding starts. You just used Claude on one email. But what if you have 15 provider responses to draft? Or 8 customer intake forms to review? Or pricing estimates for 20 jobs? This is where the economics flip — the model doesn't get tired, doesn't slow down, and costs you nothing extra.
What's something at HouseAccount where you or your team do the same type of task repeatedly? Provider outreach? Customer follow-ups? Quote reviews?

Whatever he says, sketch the pattern together:

  • Step 1: Do one manually with Claude (like we just did)
  • Step 2: Save the prompt that worked as a Project instruction or note
  • Step 3: Next time, paste in the new data and reuse the same approach
  • Step 4: Eventually, this becomes a script that Claude Code runs on a batch of inputs
You don't automate first. You do it once, learn what works, then batch it. Manual → Prompt → Batch → Automated. That's the ladder.
52–60 min Homework & Next Steps

Goal: He leaves with specific things to do this week, not just concepts.

The goal this week isn't to build anything. It's to change the reflex. Every time you catch yourself doing something that requires thinking — stop, and try handing it to Claude first. Even if it takes longer the first time. You're training the muscle, not optimizing for speed yet.

His homework (this week):

  • 3 real tasks per day in Claude. Not toy examples — actual work. Provider emails, customer analysis, process documentation, anything. Doesn't matter if the output is perfect. The reps matter.
  • Keep a "delegation log." A simple note (even in Apple Notes) — every time you use Claude, write one line: what you asked, how useful the response was (1-5), and what you'd do differently. This builds self-awareness about where AI fits in YOUR workflow.
  • Expand the HouseAccount Project. Every time Claude gives you something useful, add the relevant context back into the Project instructions. It compounds.
  • Try Claude Code on one thing. Even if it's just claude in a folder with a CSV. Ask it to analyze something. Get used to the terminal flow.

Schedule Session 2 for next week. Agenda will be driven by his delegation log — what worked, what didn't, and where to push deeper. By session 2, he'll have real opinions about where AI fits vs. doesn't in his workflow.

By next Friday, the goal is that reaching for Claude feels as natural as reaching for Google. Not because you've memorized prompts — because your brain has started to pattern-match on "this is a Claude moment."
BONUS HouseAccount-Specific Angles (Use If Time Allows)

If he mentions any of these, lean in — they're high-leverage for his business:

  • Provider matching: "Imagine feeding Claude every past job — type, zip code, rating, price, completion time. It can draft matching logic that's better than a rules engine because it understands nuance."
  • Intake scoping: "The Customer Intake Agent you've scoped — Claude is already great at this. Give it a photo + description + job type and it'll produce a structured scope of work. You could prototype that in Claude Desktop today."

  • The Concierge: "This is what OpenClaw becomes later. A persistent, always-on presence that knows HouseAccount's context and can handle homeowner questions, route to the right provider, escalate when needed."
Don't go too deep on the agent architecture today. He needs the mental model and the daily habit first. The agent map is a Session 3-4 conversation when he has enough experience to have opinions about what should be automated.
