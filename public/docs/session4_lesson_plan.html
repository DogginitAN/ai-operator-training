What This Session Is About

Everything you've built so far has been inward-facing — your data, your providers, your rate book. Claude has been a builder. That's powerful, but it's half the picture.

This session introduces the other half: using Claude to go outward. Fetch articles, rip transcripts, scrape competitor sites, pull industry reports — and turn raw external information into structured intelligence you can act on.

The skill we're building is the reflex itself. When you encounter something interesting — a podcast, a competitor's pricing page, an industry report, a thought leader's blog post — your first instinct should be: feed it to Claude and ask "what can I do with this?"

0–15 min The Shift: Builder → Researcher

Quick check-in on what's happened since last session — what you built, what broke, what surprised you. Then the reframe:

Up to now, every time you've opened Claude, you've had a task — build this feature, fix this bug, organize these files. That's the builder reflex, and it's working. But there's a second reflex that most people never develop: using Claude to go find things you didn't know you needed.

Think about how you consume information right now. You read an article, listen to a podcast, skim a competitor's site. Some of it sticks, most of it doesn't. The research reflex changes that: instead of passively consuming, you actively capture and structure everything through Claude. Every article becomes a briefing. Every podcast becomes searchable notes. Every competitor becomes a data point in a system.

Examples of what this looks like in practice:

  • A home services industry report (PDF) → Claude extracts the 5 data points that matter for HouseAccount's pricing model
  • A competitor's website → Claude maps their service areas, pricing structure, and positioning into a comparison doc
  • A podcast interview with a marketplace founder → Claude pulls out the operational playbook buried in 90 minutes of conversation
  • A blog post about AI in field services → Claude identifies which ideas apply to your provider matching system
15–35 min The Fetch: Live Demo

We'll pick something real and relevant to your world and process it live. The goal is to show the full loop: find something → feed it to Claude → get structured output you can use.

Option A: Article or blog post. We find a recent article about home services marketplaces, AI in field services, or marketplace economics. Paste the URL into Claude. Ask it to extract the key takeaways, identify what applies to HouseAccount specifically, and flag any competitive intelligence.

Option B: Podcast or video transcript. Take a YouTube video or podcast episode — a founder interview, an industry talk, a deep dive on marketplace operations. Claude can work with the transcript directly. We'll extract the operational insights and structure them as action items.

Option C: Competitor analysis. Pick a competitor or adjacent company — another home services marketplace, a handyman franchise, a provider matching platform. Feed Claude their website, their pricing page, their "how it works" flow. Get back a structured competitive brief.

We'll do this together in your Chat project. You pick the source material, we process it live. The emphasis is on the prompting pattern — how to ask Claude to extract, structure, and apply external information to your specific business context.

The prompting pattern:

Here's [article / transcript / website content]. I run HouseAccount — a home services marketplace connecting homeowners with providers. We handle job intake, provider matching, pricing, and fulfillment monitoring. Extract: 1. Key insights relevant to our business 2. Anything about pricing, provider management, or fulfillment rates 3. Ideas I should test or investigate further Format as a brief I can reference later.
The pattern scales. This same prompt structure works whether you're processing one article or fifty. Change the source, keep the frame. That's what makes it a reflex — you don't have to think about how to ask, just what to feed it.
35–50 min The Synthesis: From Raw to Useful

Fetching is step one. The real value is in what happens next — turning raw inputs into something that compounds over time.

Level 1: One-shot briefs. You read an article, feed it to Claude, get a summary. Useful, but it's a one-time thing. Most people stop here.

Level 2: Structured extraction. Instead of a summary, you ask Claude to extract specific fields — pricing data points, operational metrics, competitive positioning, customer acquisition strategies. Now it's data, not just notes. You can compare across sources.

Level 3: Running intelligence. You build a project or a file where extracted insights accumulate. Every article, transcript, and competitor analysis adds to the same structured document. Over weeks, you have a living competitive intelligence brief that no one else in home services has — because it's built from your specific lens.

The difference between Level 1 and Level 3 is the difference between reading the news and running an intelligence operation. Same input, different system. The system is just a file that Claude updates every time you feed it something new.

We'll take the output from the live demo and structure it for Level 2 — showing how the same content becomes more useful when you extract it into a consistent format rather than a narrative summary.

Reprocess the source from Block 2, but this time ask Claude to extract into a structured format: a markdown table or a JSON object with consistent fields. Compare the two outputs — the narrative brief vs. the structured extraction. Which one would be more useful if you had 20 of them?
50–65 min Build a Pipeline

A research pipeline is just the fetch-and-synthesize pattern made repeatable. We'll sketch one together that's directly relevant to HouseAccount.

Pick a research question that matters to your business right now. Some ideas:

  • Pricing intelligence: What are competitors charging for common handyman services in your markets? How does your rate book compare?
  • Provider acquisition: How do other home services platforms recruit and retain providers? What's working?
  • Fulfillment optimization: What do marketplace operators say about reducing time-to-match and improving completion rates?
  • Market expansion: Which adjacent service categories are growing fastest? Where should HouseAccount expand next?

Once you pick the question, we'll build the pipeline:

  • Sources: Where does this information live? Industry blogs, competitor sites, forums, job boards, public filings?
  • Extraction template: What fields do you want from every source? Build a consistent schema.
  • Output format: Where does the synthesized intelligence land? A markdown file in your project? A structured doc in Drive?
  • Cadence: Is this a one-time deep dive or something you'd run weekly/monthly?
This is how the research reflex becomes a system. The first time you do it manually in Chat. The second time you notice the pattern. The third time you write a skill for it. Sound familiar? Same ladder from Session 3 — repetition to automation — applied to research instead of building.

REF Quick Reference

The Research Reflex in One Line:

Encounter something → feed it to Claude → get structured output → add it to your system.

Three Levels of Research Processing:

  • Level 1 — One-shot brief: Feed source, get summary. Useful but doesn't compound.
  • Level 2 — Structured extraction: Extract into consistent fields. Now it's data you can compare.
  • Level 3 — Running intelligence: Extractions accumulate in a living document. Compounds over time.

The Prompting Pattern:

Here's [source material]. I run [your business] — [one-line description]. Extract: 1. Key insights relevant to my business 2. [Specific data points you care about] 3. Ideas to test or investigate Format as [brief / table / JSON / comparison].

Research Pipeline Components:

  • Sources: Where does the information live?
  • Extraction template: What fields do you want from every source?
  • Output format: Where does the result land?
  • Cadence: One-time or recurring?

Source Types Claude Can Process:

  • URLs (articles, blog posts, landing pages, pricing pages)
  • PDFs (reports, whitepapers, filings, franchise documents)
  • Transcripts (podcasts, YouTube videos, meeting recordings)
  • Screenshots (competitor dashboards, error messages, UI patterns)
  • Raw text (forum posts, email threads, Slack exports)
