FETCH Process 3 External Sources

Find three things relevant to HouseAccount that you'd normally skim and forget. Articles, podcasts, competitor sites, industry reports — anything from outside your own data.

STEP 1 — PICK YOUR SOURCES
They don't all need to be the same type. Try a mix — maybe one article, one competitor website, one podcast transcript or YouTube video. The only rule: it should be something that could inform a decision you're making at HouseAccount.
STEP 2 — FEED AND EXTRACT
For each source, use the prompting pattern from the session:
Here's [source — paste URL, upload PDF, or paste transcript]. I run HouseAccount — a home services marketplace connecting homeowners with providers. We handle job intake, provider matching, pricing, and fulfillment monitoring. Extract: 1. Key insights relevant to our business 2. Anything about pricing, provider management, or fulfillment 3. Ideas I should test or investigate Format as a brief I can reference later.
STEP 3 — SAVE THE OUTPUT
Don't let the briefs disappear into chat history. Save each one somewhere persistent — a markdown file in your project folder, a Google Doc, even a Jotting pinned to Session 4. The point is that you can find it again in two weeks.
Starter sources if you're stuck: Google "home services marketplace trends 2026", search YouTube for marketplace founder interviews, look at how Thumbtack or Angi describe their provider matching, or find a franchise disclosure document for a handyman franchise (they're public filings — full of operational data).
COMPETE Build a Competitive Brief

Pick 2–3 companies in or adjacent to home services. Analyze each one through Claude and compile the results into a single document you can reference.

PICK YOUR TARGETS
Direct competitors, companies you admire, or adjacent plays in home services. Could be Thumbtack, Angi, Taskrabbit, a local handyman franchise, or a vertical SaaS serving contractors. You're looking for companies where their approach to any part of the value chain — intake, matching, pricing, fulfillment — could teach you something.
ANALYZE EACH ONE
For each company, feed Claude their website (homepage, pricing page, "how it works" page) and ask for structured extraction:
Analyze [company website URL] as a competitor to HouseAccount. Extract: - Their positioning (who they serve, how they describe themselves) - Service categories and geographic coverage - Pricing model (how do they charge? flat fee, percentage, subscription?) - Provider acquisition (how do they recruit providers?) - Any notable tech or AI features - What they do better than us / what we do better
COMPILE INTO ONE DOCUMENT
Ask Claude to take the individual analyses and merge them into a single competitive landscape brief. Side-by-side comparison. This should be something you could show an investor, an advisor, or a teammate to explain where HouseAccount sits in the market.
SKILL Write a Research Skill

Take the fetch-and-synthesize pattern you practiced and turn it into a reusable skill file. Same format as Session 3.

WRITE THE SKILL
# Skill: [Name — e.g., "Competitor Analysis" or "Article Intelligence Brief"] ## When to use [When would you reach for this? What triggers it?] ## Inputs needed - [URL, PDF, transcript, etc] - [Business context if not already in project instructions] ## Steps 1. [How to feed the source to Claude] 2. [What to ask for — the extraction prompt] 3. [How to structure the output] 4. [Where to save it] ## Expected output [What the result should look like] ## Gotchas - [What goes wrong? Paywalled articles? Transcripts too long?]
TEST IT
Open a fresh Chat conversation. Paste the skill in. Give it a URL or source you haven't processed yet. Does it produce a useful result without you having to explain anything extra? If Claude asks questions, those gaps go back into the skill.
EXPLORE Find Something That Surprises You

This one's intentionally open-ended. Feed Claude a source you wouldn't normally read — something outside your usual information diet.

IDEAS
  • A franchise disclosure document (FDD) for a handyman franchise — these are public filings and contain detailed operational and financial data
  • An academic paper on marketplace economics or two-sided platforms
  • A city building department's annual report on home renovation permits in one of your markets
  • A subreddit thread where homeowners complain about hiring contractors
  • A provider's perspective — find a contractor blog or forum where they talk about what platforms they like and hate
WHAT TO LOOK FOR
You're not looking for anything specific. You're practicing the reflex of feeding something unfamiliar to Claude and asking: "what's in here that I wouldn't have noticed on my own?" The best insights often come from sources your competitors aren't reading.

  • When you read something interesting, feed it to Claude. Article in your inbox? Industry newsletter? Competitor announcement? Don't just read it — process it. Even a quick "summarize this and tell me what applies to HouseAccount" counts.

The shift this week: You've been consuming information the same way for years — skim, absorb what sticks, forget the rest. Start routing everything through Claude first. Not because you can't read — because Claude catches what you miss and structures what you'd forget.
HELP If Something Goes Wrong

"Claude can't access the URL I pasted" — Some sites block automated access. Try copy-pasting the article text directly instead. For PDFs, download and upload the file. For YouTube videos, search for the transcript and paste that.

"The output is too generic / not useful" — Your business context isn't specific enough in the prompt. Add more detail about HouseAccount — your markets, your pricing model, your current challenges. The more specific your context, the more specific Claude's extraction.

"I don't know what sources to look for" — Start with what you already consume. Industry newsletters, LinkedIn posts from people in home services, articles that cross your feed. The reflex isn't about finding new sources — it's about processing the ones you already see differently.

"The competitive brief feels shallow" — Feed Claude more pages from each competitor. Homepage alone gives you positioning. Add the pricing page, the provider signup page, their blog, their job postings (those reveal what they're investing in). More input = richer analysis.

Anything else breaks — Drop it in Jottings or bring it to the next session. That's what we're here for.
